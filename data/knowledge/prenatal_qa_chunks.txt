Q: Which poses are commonly recommended in the first trimester?
A: Many providers support gentle mobility and breath-led movement in the first trimester. Common options include mountain pose, cat-cow, child's pose, and easy seated breathing. Always follow your clinician's guidance.

---
Q: What changes in the second trimester for yoga safety?
A: The second trimester often brings a growing bump and shifting balance. Widen stances, use props, and avoid holding breath. Modify deep twists and deep backbends; prioritize stability and comfort.

---
Q: What should I avoid in the third trimester?
A: Many clinicians advise reducing long time flat on the back, strong supine work, and deep closed twists. Balance challenges and inversions may also be limited. Get personalized advice from your care team.

---
Q: Is downward dog safe during pregnancy?
A: For many people, a supported downward dog can be fine early-to-mid pregnancy. Later pregnancy may require shorter holds, higher hands, or alternatives like child's pose if you feel pressure, dizziness, or discomfort.

---
Q: Are bridges okay while pregnant?
A: Short, gentle bridges may be appropriate for some people earlier in pregnancy. In late pregnancy, supine positioning and strong backbending may need modification. Ask your clinician if you have blood pressure issues or other complications.

---
Q: What are warning signs to stop exercising?
A: Stop and seek urgent guidance for chest pain, severe shortness of breath, regular painful contractions, vaginal bleeding, fluid leakage, severe dizziness, or calf pain/swelling. This list is not exhaustive.

---
Q: How should I breathe during prenatal yoga?
A: Use smooth nasal breathing when comfortable, avoid breath-holding (valsalva), and keep a steady rhythm. If breath feels strained, simplify the pose and rest.

---
Q: What pelvic floor cues are common in prenatal yoga?
A: Many classes emphasize gentle engagement with exhale and full relaxation with inhale, coordinated with movement. If you have pelvic pain or prolapse symptoms, ask a pelvic floor specialist for individualized cues.

---
Q: Can I do twists while pregnant?
A: Gentle open twists may be preferable to deep closed twists, especially as pregnancy progresses. Keep the belly comfortable and avoid compression; props can help.

---
Q: What are benefits of prenatal yoga?
A: Potential benefits include improved mood for some people, better body awareness, gentle mobility, stress reduction skills, and community support. Benefits vary individually.

---
Q: Is hot yoga recommended in pregnancy?
A: Many guidelines suggest avoiding excessive heat exposure in pregnancy due to overheating risk. Discuss alternatives with your clinician.

---
Q: How do I modify warrior poses later in pregnancy?
A: Shorten your stance, use a wall or chair, keep ribs stacked, and avoid deep backbending in the lower back. Stop if you feel pelvic pain or instability.

---
Q: What about pigeon pose?
A: Pigeon can stress the pelvis and knees. Use bolsters, keep angles smaller, or substitute figure-four on your back if approved by your clinician.

---
Q: Are inversions safe?
A: Inversions are not appropriate for everyone in pregnancy due to balance, blood pressure, and fall risk. Many teachers recommend avoiding them unless you have specialized clearance and experience.

---
Q: What should I do if I feel dizzy in class?
A: Stop, sit or lie on your side as appropriate, hydrate, and contact your clinician if symptoms persist or worsen.

---
Q: Can yoga help with back pain in pregnancy?
A: Some people find gentle mobility and strengthening helpful, but back pain has many causes. Seek evaluation for severe pain, numbness, or weakness.

---
Q: Is child's pose always safe?
A: Widening knees can make room for the belly. If you feel pressure or nausea, rise slowly and choose a higher-supported position.

---
Q: What about seated forward folds?
A: Keep spine long, bend knees generously, and avoid forcing. Props under knees can reduce strain as pregnancy progresses.

---
Q: How do I choose a prenatal class?
A: Look for instructors with prenatal training, clear modification options, and an environment that encourages you to rest when needed.

---
Q: When should I call a doctor about exercise?
A: If you have pregnancy complications, preterm labor risk, certain heart or lung conditions, or any new symptoms, ask your clinician before starting or continuing exercise.
